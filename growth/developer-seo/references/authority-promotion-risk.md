# Authority, Promotion, and Risk

Use this reference to evaluate reputation, links, promotion, penalties, and ethical boundaries.

## 1. Authority model

Authority is topic- and context-dependent. Evaluate:

- **Relevance:** Does the site/page participate meaningfully in this subject?
- **Evidence:** Does it publish first-hand data, experience, tools, reporting, or expert analysis?
- **Recognition:** Do independent, credible sources mention, cite, review, or recommend it?
- **Entity clarity:** Are the organization, people, products, locations, and relationships consistent and verifiable?
- **Trust:** Are claims, policies, authorship, ownership, contact paths, and corrections transparent?
- **User preference:** Do people seek, revisit, recommend, and convert with the brand?

Do not collapse this into a third-party authority score. Such scores are useful comparative proxies, not search-engine truth.

## 2. Why pages earn references

Build assets worth citing or sharing:

- original studies, datasets, benchmarks, and transparent methodologies;
- expert commentary and primary-source interviews;
- definitive guides with unique examples;
- free tools, calculators, templates, visualizations, and APIs;
- newsworthy initiatives, open-source work, and public resources;
- useful comparisons, directories, or statistics maintained over time;
- strong products, service experiences, communities, and partnerships.

The promotion plan begins during asset design: identify who benefits from referencing it, why their audience cares, and what claim or utility is genuinely new.

## 3. Prospect qualification

Evaluate a potential source or partner by:

- topical and audience relevance;
- editorial standards and real readership;
- page/context fit, not domain score alone;
- ownership transparency and traffic quality;
- outbound-link patterns and sponsorship disclosure;
- likelihood of a durable relationship or useful referral traffic;
- legal, brand, and conflict risks.

Reject link farms, private networks, hacked pages, spun guest-post sites, irrelevant high-score domains, and sites selling followed links at scale.

## 4. Ethical promotion workflow

1. Identify a real audience overlap.
2. Study the person's work and the exact page/resource context.
3. Offer a specific asset, correction, dataset, expert, or collaboration that helps their audience.
4. Personalize with substance, not tokenized compliments.
5. Keep follow-ups bounded and provide an easy exit.
6. Track relationships and outcomes, not only links.
7. Disclose compensation, sponsorship, samples, and conflicts.

Do not automate human-scale relationship claims. Do not threaten, deceive, impersonate, or manufacture urgency.

## 5. Link hygiene

- Fix broken internal links and links to redirects/noncanonical URLs at source.
- Reclaim legitimate broken backlinks after migrations with relevant redirects.
- Monitor lost links to valuable assets and investigate the reason before outreach.
- Use `rel="sponsored"` for paid placements and `nofollow`/`ugc` where platform policy calls for them.
- Audit widgets, templates, affiliates, scholarships, directories, and partner badges for manipulative anchor patterns.
- Treat outbound links as editorial choices. Link to useful primary evidence without hoarding PageRank mythology.

## 6. Toxic-link and disavow decisions

Do not recommend mass disavow because a tool labels links toxic. Many low-quality links are ignored without action.

Escalate when there is evidence of:

- a manual action involving unnatural links;
- a documented history of controlled paid/link-scheme activity;
- an active campaign the organization can stop or remove;
- obvious manipulative patterns tied to the site.

Preserve evidence, attempt reasonable removal where appropriate, and use disavow cautiously. It can discard helpful signals and does not repair broader trust or content problems.

## 7. Spam and reputation-abuse review

Check for:

- doorway or location pages with the same destination and no local value;
- scaled pages generated primarily to capture queries;
- third-party sections published to exploit host reputation;
- expired-domain repurposing unrelated to prior purpose;
- hidden text, cloaking, sneaky redirects, scraped/syndicated content without value;
- fake reviews, fabricated authors, false freshness, or misleading structured data;
- affiliate pages that add no testing, comparison, or service value;
- parasite placements and link exchanges disguised as editorial work.

When found, stop the generating process, contain indexation, preserve evidence, assess scope, remediate the root incentive/workflow, and document reconsideration needs.

## 8. Traffic loss and penalty posture

Distinguish:

- manual action;
- security issue or hacked content;
- technical deindexation/canonicalization;
- migration failure;
- demand or seasonality shift;
- competitive displacement;
- SERP/AI answer click displacement;
- quality/relevance reassessment;
- analytics breakage.

Do not promise recovery dates. For manual actions, fully correct the violation, document representative evidence and systemic prevention, then submit a concise reconsideration request where supported.
